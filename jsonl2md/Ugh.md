By their own construction, nothing. That's not an oversight in the thought experiment — it's the conclusion. Chalmers saw this and named it: the paradox of phenomenal judgment. On his view everything you say about your inner life is fully explained without once invoking the inner life. So the awkward question isn't "how do I know *you're* not a zombie." It's "how does *he* know *he* isn't?" His certainty that he's conscious is, by his own lights, something a zombie would have and voice for identical reasons. The unprovability bites hardest in the first person, exactly where he felt safest.

His escape — and it's the only one — is to say consciousness isn't *detected* at all. He doesn't infer it from behavior or read it off an instrument; he's *acquainted* with it, and acquaintance is a way of knowing that doesn't run through causal contact. Which is the bedrock we hit last time. The whole position rests on acquaintance being a genuine epistemic relation that isn't detection. Grant that and you can't touch him.
